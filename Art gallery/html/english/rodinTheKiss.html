The Kiss



AuthorAuguste Rodin
Estimated value2 500 000$
Estimated age141 years



Auguste Rodin's sculpture "The Kiss" was created in 1882. It was originally conceived as part of a larger commission called "The Gates of Hell," which was inspired by Dante's Divine Comedy. "The Kiss" was initially designed to represent the adulterous lovers Paolo and Francesca, who are condemned to the second circle of Hell. However, Rodin decided to separate the sculpture from the larger composition and present it as an independent artwork.

"The Kiss" portrays a couple locked in a passionate embrace, their bodies entwined in a moment of intimate connection. The sculpture showcases Rodin's exceptional ability to capture the human form in a state of emotional intensity. The figures' limbs merge and twist, creating a dynamic composition that exudes sensuality and desire. The sculpture's timeless theme of love and the universal experience of physical and emotional connection continue to resonate with viewers across generations.

Rodin's innovative approach to sculpture is evident in "The Kiss." He deliberately left the figures partially unfinished, with rough surfaces and visible tool marks, which adds a sense of realism and immediacy to the piece. The contrast between the smooth and polished areas of the figures' bodies and the roughness of the rock on which they are positioned creates a striking visual effect, emphasizing the intensity of the lovers' embrace.

In conclusion, "The Kiss" by Auguste Rodin, created in 1882, is a remarkable sculpture that captures the power and intensity of human love. Its dynamic composition, skillful portrayal of the human form, and Rodin's innovative technique make it an enduring masterpiece in the history of art.
